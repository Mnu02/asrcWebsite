Academic Land Grab

A Historical Farce

We have seen and thought about invasion of Native lands and celebration of Thankgiving. It is important to take the time to see how univerisities (Hello Cornell) in the US came to be and their efforts (or lack thereof) to try to make repatriations to Native People.

  • The Noble Beginnings

    the great American universities set forth on a mission. Not a mission of knowledge, mind you, but of acquisition. Armed with the Morrill Act of 1862, which was supposedly about creating educational opportunities, these institutions participated in a grand land grab that would make even the most ambitious monopoly player blush. Land, of course, was just lying around, seemingly waiting for the refined touch of academia. Who better to cultivate the wilderness than the great minds who brought you Latin diplomas and football scholarships? And so, they took—acres upon acres of Native American land, no questions asked.

  • The Artifacts Custody Battle

    Fast forward to today, and our beloved universities are still clutching onto Native American artifacts like a toddler with a toy they refuse to share. The rationale? "The Natives wouldn't know how to take care of them." Because, obviously, the people who created these artifacts centuries ago need a lesson in preservation from institutions barely two centuries old. Let's talk about the Nagpra Act of 1990, which was supposed to right these wrongs by returning sacred artifacts to their rightful owners. How's that going? About as well as a group project where one person does all the work, and the rest just watch. U.S. Senator Brian Schatz has had to step in, shaming universities into doing what should have been done decades ago.

  • The Ongoing Comedy of Error

    This whole scenario would be hilarious if it weren't so tragic. It's like watching a historical re-enactment where the invaders are celebrated, and the invaded are given a participation trophy. We name dorms with Native American names, acknowledging the lands we've taken, only to infest those dorms with mice. A fitting metaphor for the ongoing invasion, wouldn't you say? And then there's Thanksgiving—where we gather to celebrate a meal that never happened in the way we imagine, on land that was never ours to begin with. It's the ultimate historical irony, repackaged as a family holiday.

  • The Modern-Day Relevance

    So why does this matter today? Because history has a funny way of repeating itself. As we debate land acknowledgments and artifact repatriation, the underlying issues of settler colonialism persist. The past isn't just a series of events; it's a legacy of dispossession that continues to affect Native and Black communities. By holding onto these artifacts and lands, universities perpetuate the myth that Native cultures are relics of the past, rather than living, vibrant communities. The refusal to return what was taken is not just an academic oversight—it's an ongoing act of erasure and disrespect.

  • What Can We Do?

    We can start by telling these stories with the irony they deserve. By highlighting the absurdity of these actions, we can bring attention to the ongoing injustices faced by Native and Black communities. It's time for universities to do more than just acknowledge the land—they need to return it. So, let's raise a glass to the institutions that taught us everything except how to give back what they stole. Here's to hoping they learn that lesson soon.
