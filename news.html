The Ganędagǫ Daily Sun

Unwelcomed guests

Cornell University's Ganędagǫ Hall, proudly named to honor the Ganędagǫ, the Cayuga Nation, opened its doors in the fall of 2021, symbolizing a sleek, modern tribute to Indigenous heritage. But as students returned this spring, they found unexpected and uninvited guests had colonized their rooms: mice. The infestation, arriving just after two arson incidents, was an unsettling reminder of another invasion centuries ago.

When a student at Cornell University, Finn Kennedy '25, returned on January 23, he hoped to ease back into his routine, only to be greeted by an invader rustling through his trash can. “I quickly turned on my flashlight, and I saw a mouse jump out and fall on the floor,” Kennedy said. He and his suitemates named the intruder Remy, after the rat from Ratatouille, and even left out a cookie for him. Cute, isn't it?

However, not all students welcomed these new inhabitants. Camila Monter '25, who is allergic to rodents, found herself sneezing and coughing. “The next day, I saw a mouse running into my roommate's closet. That's when I realized they were the cause of my allergic reaction,” Monter said. The university's response? Simple: be cleaner. Executive Director of Housing & Residential Life, Tim Blair, advised students to manage their trash better and store food properly.

Perhaps, the students were merely reclaiming the space that once belonged to them, metaphorically speaking. After all, what's a little dispossession among friends? Cornell's very grounds whisper tales of the Ganędagǫ, from whom this land was taken. The mice, in their humble way, remind us of the original inhabitants who were forced out by more than just trash management issues.

Monter and others, neat and tidy as they are, disagree with the cleanliness argument. “My roommate and I would never leave food on the floor,” Monter insisted. Yet, the mice persisted, undeterred by human claims of neatness.

Residents expressed frustration at the university's sluggish response. “The University should have acted more swiftly,” Kennedy said. Instead, students endured weeks of waiting for mice traps, all while juggling classes and seeking temporary housing. Monter detailed her ordeal of finding a place to stay, managing the first week of classes, and decontaminating her belongings—all at her own expense.

The irony is palpable. Here, in Ganędagǫ Hall, students face an invasion that mirrors the original conquest of Native land. The mice, unwelcome and persistent, evoke a historical parallel, turning the residents into modern-day "natives" besieged by an invasive force. As the students of Cornell struggle against their tiny invaders, perhaps they can pause to reflect on the larger story of displacement and invasion and the ongoing legacy of settler colonialism. After all, isn't it just a matter of perspective who the true invaders are?

What you should think about...

  • Displacement

    The mice, symbolizing the settler, remain resilient despite human attempts to eliminate them. This invites the audience to consider that the displacement of Indigenous peoples is not merely a past event but an ongoing struggle. Native communities persist in asserting their presence and rights, enduring centuries of marginalization and continuing to challenge their erasure.

  • Response and Responsibility

    The university's response to the infestation, which shifts the responsibility for cleanliness onto students, mirrors a broader tendency of institutions to deflect accountability and oversimplify complex issues. This approach reflects how institutions often address Indigenous concerns, providing superficial solutions without addressing the deeper historical and social contexts.

  • Invasion perspective

    The final question—who the true invaders are—invites the audience to rethink their understanding of colonization and displacement. By comparing the students' experience with mice to the historical plight of Indigenous peoples, the text challenges readers to empathize with the Native perspective and acknowledge the lasting impact of settler colonialism.
